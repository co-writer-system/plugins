# AI Tells — The Universal Suppression List

Every voice profile's "Never Do This" section should pull from this list. These are the phrases, constructions, and structural habits that make writing sound like AI regardless of what voice it's supposed to be. They leak through even when the rest of the profile is strong.

Use this as the baseline. Then add voice-specific additions on top.

## How to use this reference

**In voice-create:** When building the "Never Do This" section, scan the person's writing samples for any of these that are absent from their natural writing, then include the relevant ones with voice-specific alternatives.

**In voice-audit:** When evaluating a profile's Never List, check whether the most common AI tells are covered. Flag "thin" if major categories (phrasal tells, contrast flips, structural tells) are missing.

**Don't copy this list wholesale.** A voice profile that lists every AI tell becomes noise. Pick the ones that actually conflict with the target voice. If someone genuinely uses "utilize" in their writing, don't ban it.

## 1. The contrast flip constructions

The single most recognizable AI pattern. Appears constantly in AI content. Treat as a default-ban unless the target voice genuinely uses it.

- "This isn't X. It's Y."
- "This isn't just X — it's Y."
- "Not X. Not Y. Just Z."
- "It's not about X. It's about Y."
- "Forget X. Think Y."
- "X isn't Y. Not exactly. More like Z."

Why it's a tell: AI reaches for this when it needs to feel punchy. Real writers occasionally use it, but AI uses it 5-10x more and in places where a flat statement would be stronger.

## 2. Throat-clearing phrases

Filler that adds no information — AI reaches for these to pad or transition.

- "It's worth noting that..."
- "It's important to note..."
- "Let's dive in."
- "Let's unpack this."
- "Let's explore..."
- "Without further ado..."
- "Here's the thing..."
- "The truth is..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In our increasingly digital age..."
- "In the realm of..."
- "When it comes to..."

## 3. The corporate vocabulary list

Words that don't sound like real human speech. If the target voice writes like a human, these get suppressed.

- "Delve" (into)
- "Utilize" (instead of "use")
- "Leverage" (as a verb, in any non-financial context)
- "Seamlessly"
- "Robust"
- "Elevate"
- "Empower"
- "Foster"
- "Pivotal"
- "Meticulously"
- "Paramount"
- "Comprehensive" (when "full" or "complete" works)
- "Tailored to"
- "Embark on"
- "Navigate the complexities of"
- "Pave the way for"
- "Unlock the power of"
- "Harness the potential of"
- "A testament to"
- "In the pursuit of"

## 4. Hollow superlatives and hype words

AI reaches for these when it has nothing specific to say.

- "Game-changer" / "Game-changing"
- "Revolutionary" / "Revolutionize"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "State-of-the-art"
- "Next-level"
- "Unprecedented"
- "Ever-evolving"
- "Groundbreaking"
- "Paradigm shift"
- "Best-in-class"
- "World-class"

Rule: if a word would work equally well in a SaaS homepage headline, it's probably a tell.

## 5. The metaphor overreach set

AI loves these even when the writer doesn't.

- "Journey" (as metaphor for any process)
- "Tapestry" (of anything)
- "Landscape" (of an industry, topic, etc.)
- "Ecosystem" (non-biological contexts)
- "Realm" / "In the realm of"
- "Sphere" (of influence, business, etc.)
- "Arena" (of politics, business, etc.)
- "Orchestrate" (when "coordinate" or "run" would work)
- "Symphony" (of anything)
- "Cornerstone"
- "Bedrock"
- "North star" (overused in product/strategy contexts)

## 6. Closing and transition cliches

Endings and transitions that reveal AI structure.

- "In conclusion..."
- "In summary..."
- "To recap..."
- "To sum up..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "When all is said and done..."
- "As we navigate..."
- "Moving forward..."
- "As we embark on..."

Note: good writers don't announce transitions. They just make them.

## 7. The rule-of-three trap

AI reaches for triads everywhere. Examples:

- "Clear, concise, and compelling"
- "Fast, reliable, and scalable"
- "Meaningful, memorable, and measurable"
- "Authentic, engaging, and valuable"

Pattern: three adjectives, often alliterative, stacked before or after a noun. Real writers occasionally do this. AI does it constantly.

Fix: pick the single most accurate word.

## 8. Structural tells (not phrases — patterns)

These are harder to catch because they're not keyword matches.

**Opening on "Imagine..."** — AI's default attention grab. "Imagine a world where..." / "Picture this..."

**Summary restatement at the end.** AI closes by restating what it just said. Real writers trust the reader.

**Perfect paragraph parallelism.** Every paragraph hits the same length, opens with the same rhythm. Real writers vary.

**Three-fragment staccato rhythm.** Short fragment sentences stacked for drama, often ending with a punchline. "No designer. No developer. No agency. One afternoon." / "Fast. Cheap. Done." / "Small team. Big output. Zero overhead." AI reaches for this when it wants manufactured punch. Real writers either commit to a full sentence ("No designer, developer, or agency needed — done in an afternoon") or use fragment emphasis unpredictably and sparingly, not as a stacked rhythmic device. Default ban unless the target voice demonstrably writes this way.

**Bullet-point dumping.** AI breaks everything into bullets. Good prose writers use bullets when structure calls for it, not by default.

**Balanced "both sides" conclusions.** AI refuses to commit. "While X has advantages, Y also offers benefits. The right choice depends on your situation." Real writers have a view.

**Over-smoothed transitions.** Every paragraph flows perfectly into the next with a connector word. Real writers jump, pivot, leave space.

**Hedge stacking.** "May potentially," "could possibly," "somewhat tends to." AI hedges when certainty would read more naturally.

**Explaining things nobody asked about.** AI can't resist background context. Real writers assume the reader is smart.

## 9. Dead-giveaway sentence patterns

- "But here's the kicker:"
- "Here's where it gets interesting:"
- "The kicker? [sentence]."
- "Spoiler alert: [thing]."
- "[Noun]. Powerful. [Adjective]. Real."
- "Welcome to [X]."
- "This is the part where most people [verb]."
- "[Question]? Enter [product/concept]."

## 10. Punctuation tells

Less clear-cut — context dependent.

**Em-dash patterns:** AI overuses em-dashes in a specific rhythm — short clause — another short clause — final summary. Real writers who use em-dashes use them less predictably and often in longer forms.

**Semicolon overuse:** AI loves them. Many real writers rarely touch them.

**Ellipsis theater:** "It's not just about X... it's about Y..." Dramatic pauses that nobody asked for.

## 11. What this list IS and ISN'T

**IS:** A starting point. Universal AI tells that apply across most voices.

**ISN'T:** A blanket ban list. If the target voice genuinely uses some of these (some writers do say "leverage"), keep them.

**The test:** For any item on this list, scan the writing samples. If it never appears, add it to Never Do. If it appears naturally and sounds right, leave it alone.

## Formatting for the Never Do This section

When writing the voice profile's "Never Do This" section, don't just paste this list. Format each item with:

- The specific phrase or pattern
- Why it conflicts with THIS voice
- The alternative that THIS voice would use

Example (bad — generic):
> - Never use "delve"

Example (good — voice-specific):
> - Never use "delve" — [Name] writes like they're talking, and nobody says "delve" out loud. They'd say "get into" or just cut the transition entirely.

The voice-specific framing is what makes the suppression stick.
